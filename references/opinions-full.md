## Op. 16-01 (2016) — Of Counsel; Fee Splitting  [Src 726; supersedes 86-03]

**Synopsis:** When a close, personal, continuous, and regular relationship exists between an "of counsel" lawyer and a firm, the "of counsel" lawyer is considered a member of the same firm for purposes of ER 1.5(e) (fee splitting) and for conflicts/imputation purposes (ERs 1.10, 1.11, 1.12). Opinion 86-03 is superseded.

**FACTS.** This opinion reexamines the conclusion of Op. 86-03 that a lawyer who is "of counsel" to a firm must comply with ER 1.5(e) regarding fee splitting, and considers what constitutes an "of counsel" relationship and whether conflicts are imputed. The committee issued this opinion sua sponte.

**QUESTIONS PRESENTED.** (1) When will an "of counsel" lawyer be considered a member of the same firm for purposes of ER 1.5(e)? (2) If an "of counsel" relationship exists, will conflicts of interest be imputed?

**APPLICABLE RULES.** ER 1.0(c) ("Firm" definition); ER 1.5(e) (division of fees between lawyers not in the same firm — proportional services or joint responsibility; client's written agreement; reasonable total fee); ER 1.10(a) (imputation); ER 1.11(a) and ER 1.12(c) (former-government and former-judge screening).

**OPINION.** The term "of counsel" is not precisely defined and encompasses a variety of situations (e.g., a practitioner associated on a different basis; a semi-retired partner available for consultation; a probationary partner-to-be; a lawyer in permanent status between partner and associate). ABA Formal Op. 90-357. The hallmark of a true "of counsel" relationship is that it is **close, personal, continuous, and regular.** Ariz. Op. 87-24; ER 7.5, Comment 3. "Of counsel" does not include a relationship involving only an individual case (e.g., local counsel), the mere forwarding/receipt of work, an outside consultant, or occasional collaboration. (I) When the relationship is truly "of counsel," the lawyer should be considered a member of the same firm for ER 1.5(e), consistent with several jurisdictions (Maine Op. 175 (2001); Virginia Op. 1866 (2012); Texas Op. 450 (1987); NYC Bar Op. 1996-8; Ohio Op. 2004-11) and Restatement (Third) § 47 cmt. g. (II) When lawyers are not in the same firm, ER 1.5(e)'s requirements apply regardless of title; whether a "firm" exists depends on the specific facts (ER 1.0(c) and Comment 2). (III) Because a true "of counsel" lawyer is "associated in" the firm, conflicts are imputed under ERs 1.10, 1.11, and 1.12; "each lawyer is vicariously bound by the obligation of loyalty owed by each lawyer with whom the lawyer is associated" (ER 1.10, Comment 2). An infrequent or screened association may avoid imputation (see Ariz. Op. 97-09), but labels do not control.

**CONCLUSION.** A true "of counsel" lawyer is considered a member of the same firm for fee-splitting under ER 1.5(e), and the relationship results in imputation of conflicts under ERs 1.10, 1.11, and 1.12. Ariz. Op. 86-03 is superseded.

## Op. 97-09 (1997) — Conflicts; Imputed Disqualification; Temporary/Contract Lawyers; Former Clients  [Src 485]

**Synopsis:** A lawyer who works on a contract basis for more than one firm will be considered an associate of each firm for conflicts and confidentiality purposes **unless** (1) a written agreement limits the scope of the contract lawyer's work to a particular client/project (specifying termination at the project's end, a date, or an event), **and** (2) the contract lawyer does not have general access to firm files. [ERs 1.6, 1.7, 1.8, 1.9, 1.10]

**QUESTIONS.** (1) Whether a contract attorney who provided discrete research for co-counsel for a city is later disqualified from contract work for another lawyer suing the city. (2) Whether a contract lawyer is an "associate" of each firm for conflicts purposes.

**OPINION.** The terms of the contractual relationship determine the scope of representation and the conflict analysis. Under ER 1.7(a), a lawyer may not represent a client directly adverse to another current client without informed consent; if the contract lawyer's relationship with the first firm is ongoing and that firm still represents the city, adverse work would require both clients' consent (relatedness is irrelevant). If the prior relationship has concluded, ER 1.9 governs former clients: no representation in the same or substantially related matter materially adverse to the former client without consent (1.9(a)), and no use of information to the former client's disadvantage (1.9(b)). Assuming the two city matters are not substantially related, 1.9(a) would not bar the work, but 1.9(b) would prohibit it if confidential information acquired could be used to the city's disadvantage, absent consent.

On the broader question, the contract lawyer is "associated" with a firm for the duration of the contract. If the contract is open-ended/not project-related, ER 1.10(a) imputation applies. If the relationship concludes at each project's end, the lawyer is treated as moving between firms and ER 1.10(b) applies (the new firm is barred only as to the same or substantially related matter in which the lawyer had acquired material confidential information). The Rules take a **functional approach** to vicarious disqualification, turning on access to confidential information (ER 1.10 Comment). A lawyer with general file access is presumed privy to all clients' information; a lawyer with limited access is presumed privy only to clients actually served.

**Prescriptive guidelines** to avoid treating the temporary lawyer as a full associate: (1) a written agreement clearly defining the scope (specific project/client; termination by completion, date, or event); and (2) a working relationship and screening that denies the temporary lawyer general access to firm files. Absent **both**, a contract lawyer is considered a full associate of each hiring firm for conflicts and confidentiality.

## Op. 97-05 (1997) — Confidentiality; Attorney-Client Privilege (third persons present)  [Src 481]

**Synopsis:** The presence of a third person — interpreter, parent, or advocate/counselor — during an attorney-client conversation does **not** affect the lawyer's duty of confidentiality under ER 1.6 (a duty far broader than the evidentiary privilege). [ERs 1.4, 1.6]

**OPINION.** Confidentiality (ER 1.6) and the attorney-client privilege (A.R.S. §§ 12-2234 (civil), 13-4062(2) (criminal)) are separate concepts; the privilege concerns compelled evidence, while the rule of confidentiality applies in all other situations. The privilege question is one of law beyond the committee's jurisdiction. ER 1.6's confidentiality obligation is "extremely broad," applying to **all information relating to the representation, whatever its source** — even information acquired before/after the relationship, that is publicly available, or learned from sources other than the client. Ariz. Ops. 95-02, 92-02, 87-22. Because the duty attaches regardless of the presence of strangers, the presence of an interpreter (necessary under ER 1.4 to communicate with a foreign-speaking client), a parent (for a minor), or a counselor/advocate (for a client in need) has **no effect** on the confidentiality obligation; all such information remains confidential under ER 1.6. The lawyer must ensure the third person understands the confidentiality requirement. As to privilege (a legal question), prudence dictates allowing third persons only when truly necessary and explaining the unsettled law to the client.

## Op. 09-03 (2009) — Trust Accounts; Safekeeping Property; out-of-state trust account; IOLTA  [Src 703]

**Synopsis:** An Arizona-licensed lawyer who maintains an Arizona office but whose firm also has an office in another jurisdiction may keep trust funds in a trust account held outside Arizona **provided the client/third person consents** and the account is at an approved financial institution. If it is a pooled trust account on which interest is not paid to clients, the interest/dividends on the Arizona lawyer's funds must be paid to the **Arizona Foundation for Legal Services and Education** (IOLTA). [ER 1.15; Ariz. R. Sup. Ct. 43]

**RULE.** ER 1.15(a): client/third-person funds "shall be kept in a separate account maintained in the state where the lawyer's office is situated, **or elsewhere with the consent of the client or third person.**" Ariz. R. Sup. Ct. 43 governs trust-account location (43, controlled by ER 1.15(a)), authorized financial institutions (43(g)), the IOLTA pooled-account/interest-remittance regime (43(f)(2)), and applicability (43(j): every Arizona-admitted lawyer must comply as to funds held in Arizona and funds held on behalf of an Arizona client/third person).

**OPINION.** Reading ER 1.15(a)'s disjunctive "or," "elsewhere" means outside the lawyer's office state. With client/third-person consent, the Arizona lawyer may hold funds in a trust account outside Arizona, but must still comply with Rule 43: the out-of-state account must be at a Rule 43(g)-authorized institution, and if pooled under Rule 43(f)(2), interest/dividends on the Arizona lawyer's funds must be paid to the AZFLSE. Because that interest must be separately calculable and remitted, it may be impractical to pool the Arizona lawyer's funds with non-Arizona lawyers' funds; a segregated Rule 43(f)(2) account dedicated to the Arizona lawyer's funds is the practical solution. Written client consent is a best practice but ER 1.15(a) does not require it (contrast ER 1.7(b), which requires written confirmation).

**CONCLUSION.** With consent, the Arizona lawyer may use the non-Arizona office's client trust account; that account must be at an approved institution (Rule 43(g)) and the interest that would otherwise be held in an Arizona account must be identified and paid to the AZFLSE (Rule 43(f)(2)).

*(Note: A trust account is required only when the lawyer holds funds of a client or third party. ER 1.15(a).)*
